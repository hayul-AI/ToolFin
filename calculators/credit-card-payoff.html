How Credit Card Interest Works

Credit cards use a revolving balance system, meaning you can borrow up to a certain limit, pay it back, and borrow again. Interest is typically calculated based on your "average daily balance" and is compounded monthly.

Compounding interest means you are charged interest on your original balance plus any interest that has already accumulated. This can cause debt to grow rapidly if only minimum payments are made.

Minimum payments are usually designed to cover the interest plus a very small percentage of the principal. By paying more than the minimum, you reduce the principal balance faster, which in turn reduces the amount of interest charged in future months.

FAQ

Is this an exact financial quote?

No. This calculator provides a mathematical estimate based on the fixed numbers you provide. Real-world balances may fluctuate due to new purchases, varying interest calculation methods, or penalty fees.

Does ToolFin connect to banks?

No. ToolFin is a static educational tool. We do not access, store, or transmit your personal financial data or bank account information.

Why does interest accumulate quickly?

Because of compounding. If your monthly payment is close to the amount of interest charged, very little of your money goes toward the actual balance, leaving nearly the same amount to be taxed with interest again the following month.

Does paying more reduce interest?

Yes. Any amount paid above the interest charged reduces the principal balance. Since interest is calculated as a percentage of the remaining balance, a lower principal results in less interest being charged in the next cycle.
